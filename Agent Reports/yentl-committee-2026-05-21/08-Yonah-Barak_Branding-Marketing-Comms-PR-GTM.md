## Positioning Diagnosis

Yentl's strongest positioning is not generic `AI fact-checker`. That category is crowded, controversial, and hard to trust. The stronger lane is:

**Yentl is a live media-literacy companion that listens beside a source, flags checkable claims, shows evidence strength, and teaches users how the rhetoric is working.**

The defensible wedge is same-context analysis: video/page remains visible; Yentl appears beside it; the user sees transcript, claims, markers, evidence, and uncertainty without leaving the source. This is meaningfully different from paste-a-claim chatbots or enterprise misinformation dashboards.

## Strengths

The name is memorable. The visual tone is distinctive. The mission fits Project Witness: critical thinking, rhetoric, antisemitism/dissent distinction, Holocaust denial handling, source discipline, and media literacy. The extension path gives a promotional hook: `Yentl beside any video`.

The corpus assets create credibility if presented honestly. A launch page can say Yentl is being tested against 200 curated videos across discourse types, with WER and judgment-under-pressure rubrics, but it must not claim accuracy that has not been proven.

## Severe Gaps

The public page is not a full product page. It lacks demo proof, five ways in, live examples, use cases, trust posture, pricing, FAQ, extension install explanation, and limitations.

The brand voice is not locked. It shifts between product, prototype, compliance, and internal validation language. That will hurt PR because journalists and educators look for inconsistency.

The product cannot yet support bold marketing claims. Security, consent, account/privacy truth, extension proof, and evaluation gaps are launch blockers.

There is no visible launch funnel: no waitlist, no demo request, no newsletter, no educator/journalist landing route, no pricing page, no press kit, no social proof, no support/contact route.

## Audience Segments

1. Educators/media literacy programs: want lesson-safe demos, classroom control, exportable reports, and pedagogy.
2. Journalists/researchers: want source-backed claim extraction, transcript navigation, export, and audit trail.
3. Civic organizations/nonprofits: want training, monitoring, and public conversation review.
4. General users: want a side panel for videos/podcasts, but they are high-risk because they may overtrust results.
5. Enterprise/brand/security buyers: adjacent, but not the first launch unless Yentl builds monitoring and dashboards.

## Recommendations

Launch positioning should be `private beta / research preview`, not `truth machine`. Use transparent language: `Yentl is wrong sometimes. It shows sources so you can inspect the evidence.`

Build a real product page with: hero, live demo clip, five source paths, extension preview, how checks work, limitations, privacy, pricing, FAQ, educator/journalist use cases, and request-access CTA.

Create launch assets: 90-second demo video, 30-second social clips, screenshot pack, founder note, FAQ, methodology one-pager, privacy one-pager, extension install guide, educator guide, press kit, and public roadmap.

PR angle: `a media-literacy companion for live video, built from a corpus of messy public discourse`. Avoid `AI determines truth in real time`.

Social strategy: short `Yentl catches the moment` clips showing a claim, source evidence, and rhetoric marker. Avoid dunk content; keep the tone calm and educational.

## Launch Blockers

No public marketing until product truth pages align with runtime behavior; contact/support exists; pricing/cost controls exist; extension proof exists; hard consent exists; and the public demo is safe and deterministic.
