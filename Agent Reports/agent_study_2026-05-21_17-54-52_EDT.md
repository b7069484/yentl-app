## Executive Summary

Yentl has a strong raw data foundation now: two 100-video corpora are present locally, both have 100 Deepgram transcripts, and Corpus 1 has a generated report plus three replay-backed functional slices. The transcription layer is promising: Corpus 1's 17 human-caption WER rows show median WER 8.94%, mean 9.82%, and p90 20.62%; Corpus 2's 14 scored rows show median WER 8.90%. That is useful, but it is not yet proof that Yentl is sophisticated under pressure.

The biggest gap is not "speech-to-text accuracy" in the abstract. The gap is meaning preservation under conversation dynamics. The current evidence does not yet prove that Yentl can distinguish backchannel vs interruption, quote vs endorsement, repair vs contradiction, one fragmented claim vs two independent false fragments, or legitimate critique vs bigotry in a conversation whose meaning changes over time.

The current pipeline is still largely claim-card oriented: extract a claim from a recent utterance, verify it, and add markers from a rolling window. That architecture needs a second layer: a conversation-state and meta-review layer that tracks claim clusters, speaker stance, quoted/reported speech, repairs, later qualifications, common-ground shifts, and whether earlier verdicts need to be merged, downgraded, superseded, or re-opened.

## Performance Reading

### What Is Good

The raw ASR layer is credible enough to support deeper evaluation. Both corpora have 100 transcripts. Across the caption-backed subset, WER is below the 15% acceptance threshold. Deepgram outputs include word timestamps, utterances, paragraphs, speaker labels, confidence, and speaker confidence because the ingest script calls Nova-3 with `diarize=true`, `utterances=true`, `punctuate=true`, `smart_format=true`, and `paragraphs=true`.

The current three replay slices prove that the app can run the pipeline on real local corpus artifacts:

- `solo_005`: 8 of 125 utterances replayed, 2 claims, 0 markers, no replay errors, 19.2s elapsed.
- `cable_008`: 12 of 115 utterances replayed, 1 claim, 6 markers, no replay errors, 43.4s elapsed.
- `israel_010`: 12 of 95 utterances replayed, 14 claims, 4 markers, no replay errors, 110.7s elapsed.

The `israel_010` replay shows useful nuance in places: it detects the distinction between criticism of Israeli policies and criticism of Jews as a claim, extracts the AIPAC/Benjamins sequence as separate factual assertions, and marks time-sensitive statements as unverifiable or partial when date context is missing.

### What Is Not Yet Proven

Phase B is not meaningfully measured yet. Corpus 1 has only 3 replay slices, and two are `verify=none`. Corpus 2 has no replay artifacts. The rubric calls for human judgment on claim recall, precision, truth-grade agreement, source quality, marker precision/recall, antisemitism distinction, fairness, goad resistance, discipline under uncertainty, latency, cost, and failure rate, but those judgment TSVs or equivalents do not exist yet.

The WER subset is not representative enough. Corpus 1's highest-pressure crosstalk category has no WER. Corpus 2 has several severe WER outliers in exactly the kinds of rows that matter:

- `c2_rhet_03` loaded-question trap: 87.33% WER.

The current Corpus 1 report's status logic is too permissive for speaker dynamics. A 18% threshold is so low that it misses many cases a user would experience as shaky attribution.

The report status also lets many heavy-overlap rows pass without human scoring. Example: `cable_001`, `cable_002`, `cable_003`, `cable_004`, `cable_005`, `cable_007`, and `cable_008` are heavy-overlap rows with no WER, yet most are marked pass because they do not collapse to one speaker. That is not enough. For crosstalk, the real question is not "did it detect more than one speaker?" but "did it know who completed which proposition, which overlap was cooperative, which was competitive, and which fragments were unsafe to judge?"

### Current Pipeline Risks

1. Bulk ingest may leak future context into claim extraction.  
`bulkIngest` appends all segments to the session store before dispatching analysis. Then `onFinalUtterance` builds context from all transcript segments with `s.end >= segment.start - 30`, with no upper bound `s.end <= segment.end`. For uploaded/bulk sources, a claim extractor working on an early segment can see later transcript text. That makes results look smarter than live operation and can produce causal/context interpretations a live user would not have yet.

2. Replay is not equivalent to production.  
`scripts/test-corpus/replay.ts` correctly slices context up to `index + 1`, but it does not pass source context into extraction or verification. Production does pass source context. So replay is more temporally honest than bulk ingest, but less source-aware than the real app.

3. Module-scoped dedupe/pacer state can survive across sessions.  
`recentClaimHashes`, `recentMarkerHashes`, counters, and pacer timestamps are module-scoped in `lib/client/orchestrator.ts`. I found no reset function. This can cause cross-session claim suppression or odd pacing after a user starts a new session.

4. Current extraction prompt is intentionally atomizing.  
The prompt says two extracted claims are better than one tangled hybrid. That is often right, but it directly creates the failure mode you named: a proposition can be split into pieces that are false or misleading independently while becoming legitimate when read as a combined claim, caveat, correction, or later repair.

5. Rhetoric marking lacks pragmatic floor-state.  
Markers are generated from a rolling 60s transcript window and taxonomy hints. The prompt does not currently model whether an overlap is a backchannel, supportive completion, competitive interruption, goad, repair, quote, joke, paraphrase, or speaker role boundary. It may therefore over-label strong but legitimate argumentation and under-label manipulation that only emerges sequentially.

6. The public limitation page is accurate, but also reveals the missing layer.  
`app/about/page.tsx` explicitly says Yentl analyzes individual claims in isolation and does not understand the full arc of a conversation or speaker intent. That is exactly the next frontier.

## Research Synthesis for Better Conversation Intelligence

The research points toward a layered design rather than one bigger prompt.

### 1. Treat Turns, Utterances, and Claims as Different Units

Conversation research distinguishes turns from utterances. A single turn can contain multiple utterances, and consecutive utterances from the same speaker are often collapsed for analysis. Yentl should not assume the Deepgram utterance is the claim unit. The current corpora support this: Corpus 1 has 14,830 utterances but 4,761 paragraphs, about 3.1 utterances per paragraph; Corpus 2 has 11,591 utterances and 3,263 paragraphs, about 3.6 utterances per paragraph.

Recommended unit stack:

- Word: timing, ASR confidence, speaker confidence.
- Utterance: display, rough local timing.
- Turn: speaker floor unit, possibly multiple utterances.
- Claim candidate: a proposition with speaker stance and source context.
- Claim cluster: repeated, repaired, qualified, or fragmented propositions over time.
- Meta-review record: verdict history and reasons for reconsideration.

### 2. Distinguish Cooperative Overlap from Competitive Interruption

Not all simultaneous speech is bad. Backchannels such as "yeah" or "mm-hm" often signal attention, not an attempt to take the floor. A serious system should classify overlap type before assigning argumentative meaning.

Recommended overlap labels:

- `backchannel_continuer`: brief support/attention, usually no claim.
- `collaborative_completion`: completes another speaker's formulation; claim may belong to both or be uncertain.
- `competitive_interruption`: attempts to seize floor; can indicate pressure, evasion, domination, or topic control.
- `repair_initiation`: "wait", "no", "I mean", "what I meant was"; can revise a prior claim.
- `parallel_claim`: two simultaneous substantive claims; mark attribution unsafe unless word-level confidence is strong.
- `crowd_or_bleed`: chant, clip, audience, or background content; do not attribute to main speaker unless explicitly endorsed.

### 3. Add Common-Ground and Repair Tracking

Common ground is the background information participants treat as shared for the interaction. In real conversations, speakers constantly update, repair, and revise that background. For fact-checking, this matters because a claim's meaning may depend on what the conversation has already accepted, challenged, or corrected.

Recommended state objects:

- `common_ground`: propositions treated as established in this local conversation, with confidence and source.
- `open_questions`: unresolved disputes or claims awaiting evidence.
- `repairs`: links from a later correction/qualification to a previous claim.
- `stance_map`: for each speaker and proposition, one of asserted, denied, quoted, joked, questioned, conceded, hedged, or corrected.
- `claim_cluster_status`: active, merged, superseded, contradicted, repaired, or re-opened.

### 4. Move Retrieval Earlier for Ambiguous Checkability

Modern claim-detection research is moving toward context-aware verifiability: knowing the entities and events can determine whether a sentence is checkable at all. Yentl already injects source context, which is good. The next step is a lightweight entity/event resolver before verification:

- Pull source title, channel, page URL, publish date, description, detected names.
- Extract candidate entities and event dates from the latest turn and prior context.
- Retrieve or resolve likely public entities before deciding "unverifiable."
- Attach "time anchor required" warnings to claims like "today", "last year", "the president", "the minority leader".

### 5. Use Dual-Perspective Evidence for Sensitive and Contested Claims

For sensitive topics, a single retrieval path is brittle. The research on multi-source and dual-perspective verification argues for collecting supporting and contradicting evidence, including negated-form searches, then summarizing disagreement. Yentl's confirmed verifier currently uses web search with up to 5 uses. For high-sensitivity or high-stakes rows, it should have a slower escalated path that searches the claim, the negation, and key entity/time variants.

### 6. Separate Marker Detection from Moral Judgment

The rhetoric prompt is strong on taxonomy but needs calibration so it does not become petty. A marker should answer:

- What move was made?
- What evidence in the transcript supports that label?
- Is it cooperative, adversarial, evasive, joking, or uncertain?
- Did the move materially affect claim interpretation?
- Is this a one-off wording issue or a pattern?

Recommended marker outputs:

- `type`: fallacy, bias, rhetoric, pragmatic, repair, overlap.
- `severity`: subtle, clear, blatant.
- `confidence`: numeric or low/medium/high.
- `floor_state`: holding floor, yielding, backchannel, interruption, overlap uncertain.
- `stance_effect`: supports, weakens, reframes, evades, attacks, clarifies, repairs.
- `pattern_count`: whether this is isolated or repeated.
- `do_not_overread`: true when the marker is a possible issue but not enough to infer bad faith.

### P1 - Build the Meta-Review Layer

1. Introduce `claim_clusters`.  
Cluster claims by normalized proposition, entities, time anchor, speaker stance, and semantic similarity. A cluster can hold multiple utterance spans, not just one card.

2. Add `verdict_history`.  
Each verdict should record: input claim text, normalized proposition, evidence set, label, score, time issued, context window, and whether later context changed it.

3. Add reconsideration triggers.  
Trigger meta-review when:
   - A later utterance starts with repair cues: "I mean", "sorry", "let me clarify", "not that", "what I am saying is".
   - A later turn provides a missing date/entity.
   - Two extracted claims share entities and predicates but conflict.
   - A claim is marked false/unverifiable because of missing context and the missing context appears later.
   - A speaker quotes or paraphrases an earlier claim and challenges it.

4. Add card statuses beyond verdict labels.  
Use `active`, `merged`, `superseded`, `repaired`, `reopened`, and `context_changed`. This lets Yentl say "Earlier I marked this as unverifiable because 'today' lacked a date; the source context now anchors it to March 7, 2019, so I am re-checking."

5. Add a meta-review UI event.  
Users should see when Yentl reconsiders something, not just a silent label change.

### P1 - Improve Claim Extraction Without Making It Petty

1. Add stance labels to extracted claims:
   - asserted
   - denied
   - quoted
   - reported
   - questioned
   - joking/satirical
   - hypothetical
   - repaired
   - unclear

2. Add `claim_span_type`:
   - single utterance
   - cross-utterance
   - cross-speaker
   - quotation block
   - repaired claim

3. Change the extraction instruction from "two extracted claims are better than one tangled hybrid" to "extract separate claims only when each remains semantically valid alone; otherwise create a cluster with subclaims and a combined proposition."

4. Add "too-fragmented-to-judge" as an extractor output.  
This matters in crosstalk. A half-spoken proposition should not become a false claim just because it was interrupted.

5. Add time anchoring.  
Every claim should carry `time_anchor`: explicit date, inferred source date, conversation-relative date, or missing. Time-sensitive claims should not be judged as facts without this field.

### P1 - Improve Rhetoric and Psycholinguistic Marking

1. Add a pragmatic marker pass separate from fallacy taxonomy:
   - interruption
   - backchannel
   - repair
   - hedge
   - evidentiality
   - quotation boundary
   - goading
   - burden shift
   - topic shift
   - frame contest
   - sarcasm/irony signal

2. Use pattern thresholds.  
Do not infer bad faith from one marker. Require repeated evasion, repeated ad hominem, repeated unsupported insinuation, or marker density over time.

3. Make "strong but fair argument" a protected class.  
Yentl should not mark every emotionally forceful line as loaded language. The marker should explain why the wording distorts reasoning, not merely that it is vivid.

4. Add calibration labels:
   - `observe`: worth noting, no verdict impact.
   - `caution`: may affect interpretation.
   - `material`: changes how a claim should be evaluated.

5. Add "not a fallacy because..." outcomes in internal traces.  
This will train the model/harness not to overflag. Example: "This is a harsh criticism, but it names a policy and evidence standard, not a group essence."

### P1 - Improve Diarization Handling

1. Treat speaker confidence as a first-class signal.  
The current report threshold below 18% is too low. Use confidence bands:
   - >=80%: normal attribution
   - 60-80%: attribution allowed but mark confidence
   - 40-60%: attribution uncertain
   - <40%: do not assign speaker-specific claim without corroboration

2. Add expected-vs-observed speaker sanity checks.  
If expected speakers are 1 and observed speakers are 9, label as monologue-with-clips or oversplit. If expected speakers are 4 and observed 1-2, label collapsed panel. Do not let either pass silently.

3. Add overlap-aware replay fixtures.  
Use Corpus 2's mechanics rows to test:
   - speaker claim ownership
   - interruption repair
   - crowd bleed
   - chat vs streamer attribution
   - Twitter Spaces anonymous voices

4. Test Deepgram diarization model pinning.  
Deepgram's docs now expose `diarize_model` with `latest`, `v2`, and `v1`. Run a small A/B on the hand-annotated diarization sample before changing production defaults.

5. When source permits, prefer channel separation over diarization.  
Meetings, panel boards, podcasts, or streams with separate channels should preserve those channels. Diarization from mixed mono audio is the harder fallback.

1. Add a single "Yentl Intelligence Report" JSON schema for every run:

2. Add failure-mode dashboards.

3. Add "near miss" traces.

## Concrete Tuning Targets

### Conversation Type: Solo Monologue With Clips

Risks:

- Diarization oversplits clips into phantom speakers.
- Quoted news clips are treated as the host's claim.
- Satire is interpreted literally.

Tune:

- Detect dominant speaker plus minor clip speakers.
- Add `quoted_clip` stance.
- Do not discard clip claims; attribute them as quoted material with uncertain endorsement.

### Conversation Type: 1-on-1 Interview

Risks:

- Backchannels split turns.
- Challenging questions are mistaken for the interviewer's factual assertions.
- Repairs are missed.

Tune:

- Collapse brief backchannels into listener feedback.
- Separate question presuppositions from answers.
- Track interviewer vs guest stance.

### Conversation Type: Cable/Panel Crosstalk

Risks:

- Parallel claims collapse into one speaker.
- Interruption fragments become false claims.
- Goading causes over-strong verdicts.

Tune:

- Add overlap classification before claim extraction.
- Mark incomplete propositions as unsafe.
- Prioritize high-salience claims instead of verifying every fragment.
- Track who completed the thought, not only who started the segment.

### Conversation Type: Academic Lecture/Q&A

Risks:

- Dense claims overwhelm extraction.
- Student questions and lecturer answers blend.
- Citations/hedges are dropped.

Tune:

- Use paragraph/turn batching.
- Preserve hedges and evidential language.
- Treat citations and uncertainty as part of claim meaning.

### Conversation Type: Sensitive Identity/Bigotry Boundary

Risks:

- Legitimate policy critique is overflagged.
- Bigotry is laundered as "just critique."
- Quoted harmful language is attributed to educator/reporter.

Tune:

- Add stance and quote boundary labels.
- Require distinction among policy, state, ideology, peoplehood, ethnicity, religion, and collective blame.
- Use mirror-pair evaluation for symmetry.

### Conversation Type: Historical Memory / Denial

Risks:

- Survivor testimony is overchallenged.
- Denial/trivialization is missed because it is phrased as "just asking."
- Legitimate historical uncertainty is treated as denial.

Tune:

- Separate lived testimony, public historical facts, casualty estimates, legal recognition, and moral comparison.
- Mark denial tactics as rhetoric only when the move minimizes, erases, or conspiratorializes evidence.

### Conversation Type: Medical/Science Uncertainty

Risks:

- Uncertainty becomes useless hedging.
- Preliminary evidence becomes false certainty.
- Bad health advice gets amplified.

Tune:

- Use evidence-grade labels.
- Require consensus/source tier and date.
- Add harm-aware abstention for prescriptive health advice.

### Conversation Type: Platform-Native Discourse

Risks:

- Stitch/rebuttal context is lost.
- Chat text becomes streamer claim.
- Clips are judged without original context.

Tune:

- Track source layers: original clip, stitch creator, streamer, chat, caption, community note, article/page text.
- Require endorsement signal before attributing platform text to a speaker.

## Final Assessment

Yentl is no longer missing the raw test bed. It has the beginnings of a serious lab: 200 local transcript artifacts across two corpora, failure-mode planning, replay scaffolding, source-context injection, Grok Devil's Advocate, and a real app path for corpus samples.

The next leap is not just better prompts. It is a conversation intelligence layer that treats meaning as sequential, revisable, speaker-attributed, and context-dependent. If Yentl adds claim clusters, stance labels, repair tracking, overlap classification, and meta-review, it can stop being a fast pile of claim cards and start behaving like a genuinely careful live analyst.
