## Category Map

Yentl overlaps with several categories:

1. Automated fact-checking tools: claim extraction, evidence retrieval, verdicts.
2. Fact-checker workflow platforms: monitoring public debate and surfacing claims for professionals.
3. Misinformation intelligence: narrative tracking, brand/security/government monitoring.
4. Media-literacy tools/games: teaching users to spot manipulation.
5. Browser companions/extensions: in-context analysis while reading/watching.
6. Transcript/ASR tools: capture, diarization, summarization, export.

Competitors are not one-to-one. Full Fact AI is closer to professional monitoring; NewsGuard is closer to narrative/source intelligence; Google Fact Check Tools is a data/API layer; AI claim-checking startups focus on paste/URL verification; transcript tools focus on capture and summarization.

## Differentiation

Yentl's strongest differentiation is **live, same-context companion analysis for media**, especially via Chrome extension: source page remains visible, Yentl adds transcript, claim, marker, and evidence context beside it.

The second differentiation is the Project Witness lens: rhetoric/bias taxonomy, antisemitism-vs-dissent boundary, Holocaust denial sensitivity, and pedagogy. This can be a strength if handled with discipline and transparent standards.

The third differentiation is corpus-driven development. Yentl has local corpora explicitly designed around failure modes most generic demos avoid.

## Risks

The category is trust-starved. Any overclaim can backfire. Many users and journalists are skeptical of automated fact-checking, especially on politics, identity, and breaking news.

Large players can add generic `fact-check this video` features. Yentl's moat cannot be model access; it must be workflow, pedagogy, trust/audit design, source discipline, and extension UX.

Enterprise misinformation tools already track narratives at scale. Yentl should not start by competing there unless it builds dashboards, data feeds, team workflows, and analyst review.

Paste-a-claim AI tools can be cheaper and faster. Yentl's value must be source anchoring and live media context, not generic answer generation.

## Recommendations

Position as a media-literacy companion and analyst tool, not an authority. Use `inspect`, `check`, `trace`, `contextualize`, `teach`; avoid `settles`, `proves`, `truth machine`.

Compete first in three lanes:

1. Educator/journalist/researcher private beta.
2. Chrome extension for same-page video/article analysis.
3. Corpus-backed evaluation around messy discourse.

Defer enterprise misinformation monitoring until analytics, team accounts, and narrative clustering exist.

Build a public `How Yentl differs` page:

- Same-page media companion, not just pasted claims.
- Transcript-linked evidence, not detached answers.
- Rhetoric markers and learning loops, not only verdicts.
- Explicit limits and human-review rubrics.
- Sensitive-topic evaluation rather than generic benchmark bragging.

## Research Positioning

Publish Yentl as a research/education artifact first: `Can a live media-literacy companion preserve meaning under pressure?` That is more credible than claiming general automated truth detection.

## Competitive Watchlist

Track Full Fact AI, NewsGuard, ClaimBuster/academic automated fact-checking, Whisp, Factward, Facticity, browser AI assistants, transcript tools, Perplexity/ChatGPT/Gemini/xAI live search, and platform-native Community Notes. Maintain a quarterly comparison matrix covering source types, live audio, extension, evidence trace, learning design, sensitive-topic policy, export, pricing, and API/team features.
